How Does the Book Differ from the Movie?

Once Percy makes it to camp in the movie, they reveal that Percy Jackson is Posidon’s son, because he built Percy a little house at the camp with tridents as decoration. While in the book, no one knows that Percy is Posidon’s son until a trident, made from water, floats above Percy’s head to protect him against a bully, named Clarissa. Clarissa was breaking the rules in Capture the Flag. In the movie this does not happen and Clarissa does not appear.

Later, Luke, one of the camp counselors, gives Percy, Grover, and Annabeth a map to each pearl they needed to escape the underworld later when they got there. This does not happen in the book. After that, Luke gives Percy a pair of flying shoes and a shield. In the book Percy gets the flying shoes but he doesn’t end up using them, instead Grover does. Also in the book, Luke never gives them a shield.

In the movie, they go on a quest and follow the map to get the pearls, while in the book, Percy is given the pearls by a water person sent by Posidon, and they just go to the locations on the way to the underworld.

The next difference, in the film, they go to a museum to get a pearl that is in a statue, but a hydra is guarding it and they fight the hydra. In the book, none of this happens, and instead they fight a Chimera on a tower and Percy jumps off it from very high into some water. Also, in the book, there is an entire bus fight scene with the three furies; in the movie none of this happens. Another difference is that in the book, Ares hides the lightning bolt in Percy’s backpack instead of Luke hiding it in Percy’s shield at the beginning of the movie. In the movie, Ares does not appear.

In the book, where Medusa appears, Medusa disguises herself as a nice lady and gives Percy, Grover, and Annabeth delicious food. In the movie, she is immediately revealed as Medusa and does not serve food. One major difference is that when Percy saves his mom she stays behind, but in the movie, instead Grover stays behind.

After they defeat Medusa in the book, they send Medusa’s head to Olympus, and at the end of the book, they ship it back to Percy’s house and his mom uses it to turn Percy’s abusive stepdad, Gabe, into a statue and she sells it. In the movie this does not happen and Percy’s mom just kicks Gabe out. In conclusion, these are the main differences that I have taken note of between the movie and the book of Percy Jackson and the Olympians: The Lightning Thief.

What couldn’t have been removed from the book in the movie because it would ruin the main idea?

One part of the story that couldn’t have been removed from the book in the movie because it would ruin the main idea is if Percy’s mom had not been captured. The two reasons this would not work is that, first, it would get rid of the motivation that Percy has on this quest, and they would not have to go to Hades.

Language as a Tool for Social Control in “The Giver”

Language is used as a tool for social control in "The Giver". Certain words are distorted from the meaning that we attribute to them and are used to promote the rules and conventions of the community. Some of these words include, "release", "birth mother", and "assignment". In this essay, I will show how these words are being used as tools for social control.

In "The Giver" they use the term "release” on several occasions. One example is when a set of twins were born, they didn’t want two people to look the same. If they looked the same it would indicate that they were siblings and the familial bond could threaten allegiance to the community. This word in "The Giver" is at first implied as going to a new place, but then is later to be revealed as death. The reason they use it this way is to soften the difficult and upsetting implications so people won’t get angry, sad, or rebel. The word "release" in real life means to let something go or to move on. In "The Giver" they use the word "release" so people won’t get sad when the community chooses to exterminate babies, the elderly, or rebels.

Another example of language being used as a tool for social control in "The Giver" is "birth mother". "Birth mothers" in "The Giver" are people who are used to create “new children”, but these birth mothers don’t raise the children. In real life, "birth mothers" are important leaders and members in family life, not just people who give birth. In "The Giver" they say it is not a very honored "assignment" to be a “birth mother” because they have three children each and then are useless for the remainder of their lives, without further “assignment.”

The next word is "assignment" and is used in "The Giver" to mean “job” but instead of having a choice, they are assigned their roles in society. When people turn 12, they get assigned their “assignment” at a ceremony. This shows that the community controls people and what they do with their lives.

This has been my essay on how language is used as a tool for social control in "The Giver" Including some words that are different from their real world meanings and how they control people, and examples of this happening.

Salva experiences several setbacks throughout the book and they seem to serve to strengthen his character. Salva’s uncle reconnected with him when he joined the group of people traveling to the emergency camp. This gives Salva hope that his family might still be alive, and it shows that Salva is a positive person.

Later, Salva’s uncle gets shot by cruel men from the other tribe. This makes him have a strong desire to meet up with the rest of his family and motivates him to keep on going. When he finally gets to the emergency camp, people fire guns and scare them away. Salva then led a group of boys to another camp where they would be safe. This shows that Salva is a leader, even in the worst of times.

Further on in the story, Salva gets picked as a person that gets to go to America. He uses his time in America to start a charity that builds wells for people that have to walk a long way for clean water and builds schools where he is from. This shows that Salva makes good use of privileged and special opportunities. In the end, Salva finds his Mom and Dad. Everything he did was worth it.

The Maze Runner: Character Evaluation

Essay by Jonah Gann

In the beginning of the book, Thomas starts out as a frustrated newcomer, but undergoes a transformation into the main leader of the group of the “Gladers.” The events that led the other “Gladers” to trust and respect his character also invest the reader, as Thomas proves himself throughout the book.

At the start of the story, Thomas is angry and confused, but eventually begins getting to know the other boys around the Glade. Thomas is very curious about the Maze and gets an urge to become a runner in the Maze, which is not a common thing for people to want because it is very dangerous. Little was known about the Maze, but there was a general belief among the “Gladers” that there was a way out through the Maze and that it could be solved. This is the driving motivation that made Thomas want to be a runner, so he could get himself, and the “Gladers” out of the Glade and into the real world. This shows that Thomas wanted to lead so he could save everyone in the Glade.

Around the middle of the book, a girl named Teresa appears. Thomas and Teresa can communicate to each other telepathically. This motivates Thomas to think that he has a special power that can help with the mystery of the Maze. Thomas’s motivation and knowledge make him qualified as a good leader for the group.

At the end of the book, Thomas sees a connection between the shape of the Maze as a clue to getting out. The runners had made a map of the Maze and he saw that the aerial view of the shape spells out words. He realizes those words can be used as codes that they can type into a computer that is in the “griever hole,” where the monsters come out and into the maze. Thomas convinces the others that there is a way out of the Maze through the “griever hole” and convinces the other boys to follow him which was not an easy task. Not all of the boys follow Thomas, but many do and eventually they escape the maze through his plan.

All of this shows how Thomas begins his journey as a confused newcomer, and later becomes a confident leader and guides others out of the maze through embracing his abilities and beliefs.

Rob Hall is introduced as a great leader in the beginning of Into Thin Air. Do the events of the story reinforce that description or undermine it? Use specific examples to support your claim.

Another example of Rob Hall being a leader with shortcomings, is that he falls into a crevasse and gets stuck. This leads to many sherpas and climbers dying attempting to save him. This further proves that Rob Hall was inadequate as a leader for this trip, even though he appears to be a strong leader when he is introduced. I think Lewis portrayed Heaven this way to show that Heaven is like an ancient and important place that newcomers can’t tamper with. So it would make sense if CS Lewis was trying to represent heaven as something true, permanent, and everlasting.
